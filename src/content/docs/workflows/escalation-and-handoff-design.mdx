# Escalation and Handoff Design

Escalation design is where many support AI programs either become operationally trustworthy or quietly create chaos. It is not enough to generate good drafts. Teams need a reliable way to decide when automation can continue, when a human must review, and how context should move across that boundary without creating extra queue work.

## Workflow objective

The goal of a handoff workflow is simple: preserve speed while making sure the right cases reach the right human with the right context attached. Good handoff design usually improves:

- escalation correctness;
- review speed for human agents;
- continuity between AI-assisted steps and the next queue owner;
- auditability when a customer interaction becomes sensitive or high risk.

## Inputs and prerequisites

Before a support team automates handoffs, it should already have:

- a written escalation policy for account, billing, outage, compliance, or safety issues;
- ticket classes that distinguish low-risk from high-risk requests;
- approved source material that can be attached or summarized cleanly;
- queue ownership rules that define where escalated work should land.

Without those inputs, escalation logic often turns into guesswork disguised as workflow design.

## A practical handoff sequence

Most durable handoff flows follow a pattern like this:

1. classify the request and detect risk markers early;
2. decide whether the AI layer is allowed to answer, draft, or only summarize;
3. generate a concise handoff packet containing intent, history, policy context, and unresolved questions;
4. route to the correct queue with explicit reason codes;
5. let the human operator review, amend, and continue the interaction.

The strongest workflows are short, opinionated, and easy for operators to trust. A handoff packet that is too long or too vague often slows the team down.

## Human review checkpoints

Human checkpoints matter most when:

- the request involves money, service restoration, or account changes;
- the knowledge sources disagree or look stale;
- the system is uncertain about policy fit;
- the customer is already frustrated and tone management matters.

The right checkpoint is rarely “review everything forever.” It is more often “force human review on the categories where mistakes are expensive.”

## Logging and regression requirements

Every escalation workflow should create enough data to answer:

- was the case escalated at the right time;
- did the next human have the context needed to act quickly;
- did the handoff summary introduce factual drift or omit something critical;
- which routing rules are producing too many false positives or false negatives.

That logging is what lets a team tune the system instead of arguing from anecdotes.
